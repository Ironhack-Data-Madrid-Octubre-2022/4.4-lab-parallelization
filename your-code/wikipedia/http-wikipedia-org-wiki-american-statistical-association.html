\nThe American Statistical Association (ASA) is the main professional organization for statisticians and related professionals in the United States. It was founded in Boston, Massachusetts on November 27, 1839, and is the second oldest continuously operating professional society in the US (only the Massachusetts Medical Society, founded in 1781, is older). The ASA services statisticians, quantitative scientists, and users of statistics across many academic areas and applications. The association publishes a variety of journals and sponsors several international conferences every year.\n

Mission[edit]

\n

The organization\'s mission is to promote good application of statistical science, specifically to:[1]\n

\n
  • support excellence in statistical practice, research, journals, and meetings
    • \n
  • work for the improvement of statistical education at all levels
    • \n
  • promote the proper application of statistics
    • \n
  • anticipate and meet member needs
    • \n
  • use the discipline of statistics to enhance human welfare
    • \n
  • seek opportunities to advance the statistics profession
\n

Membership[edit]

\n

ASA has about 18,000 members, found in government, academia, and the private sector.[2]\n

\n

Fellowship[edit]

\n
Main article: List of Fellows of the American Statistical Association
\n

New Fellowships of the ASA are granted annually by the ASA Committee on Fellows. Candidates must have been members for the preceding three years but may be nominated by anyone. The maximum number of recipients each year is one-third of one percent of the ASA membership.[3]\n

\n

Organizational structure[edit]

\n

ASA is organized in Sections, Chapters and Committees. Chapters are arranged geographically, representing 78 areas across the US and Canada. An example of an early and large chapter is the SoCalASA. Sections are subject-area and industry-area interest groups covering 22 sub-disciplines. ASA has more than 60 committees coordinating meetings, publications, education, careers, and special-interest topics involving statisticians.\n

\n

Accredited Professional Statistician[edit]

\n

As of April\xc2\xa02010[update], the ASA offers the Accredited Professional Statistician status (PStat), to members who meet the ASA\'s credentialing requirements, which include an advanced degree in statistics or related quantitative field, five years of documented experience, and evidence of professional competence.[4] A list of current members with PStat status is available.[5]\n

The ASA also offers the Graduate Statistician status (GStat) as of April 2014.[6] It serves as a preparatory accreditation suitable for graduate students.\n

Meetings[edit]

\n

Meetings provide a platform for scholars and practitioners to exchange research, job opportunities and ideas with each other. ASA holds an annual meeting called Joint Statistical Meetings (JSM),[11] a conference on statistical methodologies and applications called Spring Research Conference (SRC),[12] Conference on Statistical Practice (CSP),[13] and sponsors multiple international meetings and special-interest group meetings.\n
